MLS Matchup Page Philosophy & UX

Project: Sports Hub / Today’s Sports

Purpose

Define what an MLS matchup page should accomplish before implementation begins.

This document intentionally contains no implementation details. It exists to guide every engineering decision that follows.

⸻

The Core Philosophy

Every sport asks a different question.

Our matchup pages should reflect that.

The goal is not to make every league look identical.

The goal is to make every league feel like experts in that sport.

⸻

MLB asks:

Which individual matchups matter most?

Baseball is driven by repeated one-on-one interactions.

A hitter versus a pitcher.

A pitcher versus a lineup.

Handedness.

Pitch mix.

Strikeouts.

Recent form.

Individual player matchups dominate the game.

The MLB page is therefore built around those relationships.

⸻

Basketball asks:

Which players are most likely to control the game?

Basketball is continuous.

The game is shaped by stars.

Usage.

Efficiency.

Rotations.

Spacing.

Pace.

The WNBA page therefore emphasizes players, lineups, and team strengths.

⸻

Soccer asks:

How is this match likely to be played?

This is fundamentally different.

Soccer is less about isolated individual battles and more about:

* systems
* tactical identity
* possession
* space
* transitions
* shape
* momentum
* moments

The purpose of the MLS page is to teach the user what kind of match they are about to watch.

⸻

The User Experience Goal

A user should be able to open the page and, within thirty seconds, understand:

* what each team wants to do
* how those styles interact
* where each team has advantages
* which players matter most
* what could decide the match

The user should leave saying:

“I understand this game.”

Not:

“I memorized twenty statistics.”

⸻

Sports Hub is not a statistics site

Statistics support the story.

They are not the story.

Every chart, bar, number, and metric should answer a question.

Never display data simply because it exists.

⸻

Every section should answer a question

⸻

Hero

Question:

What game am I looking at?

The hero immediately establishes context.

Competition.

Kickoff.

Venue.

Records.

Standings.

Weather.

Logos.

Current status.

Nothing analytical yet.

Just orientation.

⸻

Matchup Snapshot

Question:

Which team has been better?

A quick side-by-side comparison.

Goals.

Defense.

Possession.

Shots.

Passing.

Recent form.

The snapshot should summarize an entire season in less than fifteen seconds.

⸻

Tactical Matchup

Question:

How will this game probably unfold?

This is the heart of the page.

Every soccer fan naturally asks:

Who will have the ball?

Who wants possession?

Who presses?

Who sits deep?

Who attacks wide?

Who counters?

Who dominates set pieces?

Who wants chaos?

Who wants control?

The Tactical Matchup section answers these questions.

This section should become the signature feature of the MLS page.

⸻

Key Storylines

Question:

Why is this game interesting?

Storylines should never be generic.

Every storyline must be supported by actual evidence.

Examples:

* Seattle has won seven straight home matches.
* Galaxy have struggled away from home.
* Both teams create many chances but finish inconsistently.
* One team dominates aerial duels.
* One team scores frequently from corners.

The story should emerge naturally from the data.

Never invent narratives.

⸻

Projected Lineups

Question:

Who is likely to play?

The lineup should communicate:

* formation
* expected starters
* availability
* uncertainty

It should never imply certainty when the data is uncertain.

Projected lineups should be clearly labeled.

Confirmed lineups should replace projections automatically when available.

⸻

Players to Watch

Question:

Which players could decide the game?

Do not simply choose the most famous players.

Choose the players who matter in this matchup.

Different positions should be evaluated differently.

Forwards.

Creators.

Defenders.

Goalkeepers.

Each should be judged using appropriate metrics.

⸻

Attacking Profile

Question:

How does each team create goals?

Examples:

* patient buildup
* quick transitions
* crossing
* through balls
* set pieces
* penalties
* long-range shooting

This section should describe style, not merely totals.

⸻

Discipline

Question:

Could physical play affect this match?

Cards.

Fouls.

Suspensions.

Aggressive defending.

Referee tendencies in the future.

This should remain a small supporting section.

⸻

Honest Gaps

Question:

What don’t we know?

One of the defining principles of Sports Hub is intellectual honesty.

If important information is unavailable, the page should simply say so.

Examples:

* lineup not yet confirmed
* injury status uncertain
* advanced tracking unavailable
* insufficient sample size

Never pretend certainty.

⸻

Soccer is about identity

One of the defining concepts of the MLS page is team identity.

Every team should gradually develop an identity profile.

Examples:

Seattle Sounders

* patient possession
* strong defensive structure
* dangerous from wide areas
* organized press

LA Galaxy

* vertical attacks
* dangerous in transition
* vulnerable defending crosses

Austin FC

* aggressive high press
* heavy possession
* attacks through midfield

These identities should be earned from data.

Not manually written.

⸻

Style is more important than rankings

Rankings are descriptive.

Style is predictive.

Two teams with identical records can produce completely different matches.

The page should prioritize understanding style over displaying standings.

⸻

Confidence matters

Every analytical statement should have an implied confidence level.

High confidence:

Large sample.

Consistent trend.

Strong evidence.

Lower confidence:

Small sample.

Recent tactical change.

Projected lineup.

Limited data.

The interface should communicate uncertainty naturally.

⸻

Less is more

Do not attempt to display every available statistic.

Every section must earn its place.

If a section does not improve the user’s understanding of the match, it should be removed.

⸻

Design Principles

The interface should feel:

* editorial
* premium
* calm
* modern
* trustworthy

It should never resemble:

* a spreadsheet
* a betting site
* a fantasy dashboard
* a database dump

White space is valuable.

Typography matters.

Visual hierarchy matters.

The page should feel curated.

⸻

What makes Sports Hub different

Most sports sites answer:

What happened?

Sports Hub answers:

Why does this matter?

That difference should be visible on every page.

⸻

The North Star

If a fan watches the match after reading this page, they should recognize the patterns they were told to watch for.

They should notice:

* the pressing
* the buildup
* the counterattacks
* the dangerous players
* the tactical adjustments

The page should make the sport easier to understand without oversimplifying it.

If a user finishes reading and says,

“Now I know what I’m looking for tonight,”

then the MLS matchup page has succeeded.
